# Chapter Question Bank

Questions derived from the game concept template (策划总纲模板). The concept
interviewer agent uses these as the basis for its "question then generate"
workflow. Questions should be adapted based on the active genre profile's
questioning emphasis.

For each chapter, the agent asks 3-5 of these questions (adapted to the genre),
waits for the user's answers, then generates the full chapter with strong
proposals and rationale.

---

## Chapter 1: Target Users

1. Who are your primary and secondary target users? Describe their age range, gender leanings, gaming experience level, preferred session length, and device/platform.
2. What are the 3 core user needs this game satisfies? Cover at least one emotional need (relaxation, thrill, achievement), one operational need (easy controls, quick sessions), and one outcome need (progression, collection, competition).
3. Describe 3 typical play scenarios (commute, bedtime, waiting room, etc.) with session length, device context, sound environment, and network condition for each.
4. What are the top 3 pain points in competing games that your target users experience? For each pain point, how does your game address it?
5. Why would a user download this game (first impression trigger)? And separately, why would they play past level 3 (early retention hook)?

## Chapter 2: Product Positioning

1. In one sentence (under 40 words), what is this game? Include the theme, core mechanic, target experience, and who it is for.
2. What are your 3-5 core experience keywords (e.g., satisfying, strategic, relaxing, intense, collectible)? For each, explain how it manifests in gameplay.
3. What are your top 3 differentiating features that a player would notice? For each, why do competitors lack it or do it worse?
4. Name 2-3 direct competitors. For each, what is your game's specific advantage in gameplay feel, theme, monetization, or content?
5. What does this game explicitly NOT include? List 3-5 scope boundaries (e.g., no social features, no real-time PvP, no heavy narrative).

## Chapter 3: Core Gameplay

1. What does the player physically do most often in a single session? Describe the 1-3 core operations with input method, trigger condition, success feedback, and failure feedback for each.
2. Walk through one complete session from start to finish -- what does the player see at entry, what actions do they repeat, what pressure point appears, and how does the session end?
3. At what moments does the player make meaningful decisions, and what do those decisions affect? Is there a trade-off or is there a clearly optimal choice?
4. When the player fails, what do they see and feel? Why would they choose to retry instead of quitting? What "almost succeeded" hook exists?
5. When does the core fun moment first appear? What specific action triggers it, and what feedback makes it feel satisfying?

## Chapter 4: Game Loops

1. What is the core loop? Describe it as a chain (e.g., "play level -> earn currency -> upgrade ability -> play harder level") and explain what changes after each cycle.
2. Within a single session, what is the in-game loop the player repeats? How often does it cycle, and what keeps each cycle from feeling identical?
3. What progression exists outside of individual sessions? At 3 sessions, 10 sessions, and 30 sessions, what has changed for the player?
4. List your core resources (ideally 3-5). For each, where does it come from, where does it go, and what happens when the player runs out?
5. How do ads and/or payments integrate into the resource loop? At what point in the loop does the player encounter monetization, and what value does it provide?

## Chapter 5: Levels & Content

1. How is content structured? Is it linear levels, themed chapters, procedurally generated, or a mix? How many levels per chapter, and how long is each level?
2. What are the different level objective types (e.g., score target, clear obstacles, time limit, collection goal)? How are they distributed across the first 30 levels?
3. What new mechanics, obstacles, or events appear as the player progresses? List at least 5 key mechanics with when they first appear and what experience they add.
4. What keeps content from feeling repetitive after 20+ sessions? What is your "minimum change unit" between levels -- what varies and what stays the same?
5. Describe your first 30 levels at a high level: what do levels 1-5 teach, levels 6-15 introduce, and levels 16-30 challenge?

## Chapter 6: Difficulty & Balance

1. What are the 3 most important parameters that affect whether a player passes or fails (e.g., move count, time limit, health, damage, resource amount)?
2. Describe your difficulty curve in stages: what is the experience target and expected pass rate for the teaching phase (levels 1-10), building phase (11-20), and pressure phase (21-30)?
3. When does the player first experience failure? Is this failure moment designed intentionally? What makes them feel it was their fault, not the game's?
4. How do you handle difficulty-related resource pressure? Is running out of resources a designed light pressure (with ad/IAP relief valve) or an unacceptable dead-end?
5. When data shows a level is too hard or too easy, what parameter do you adjust first, and why? What parameters should never be adjusted without reconsidering the design?

## Chapter 7: Onboarding

1. In the first 10 seconds after opening the game, what does the player see, and what is the single action they are guided to perform?
2. By the end of the first minute, what rules has the player learned, and what positive feedback have they received?
3. By the 3-minute mark, has the player completed one full success loop, experienced one satisfying moment, and received one hint about what comes next?
4. When does the first ad appear, and why is that the right moment? What would happen if it appeared one step earlier or later?
5. When does the first paid item or remove-ads offer appear? Why has the player built enough understanding by that point to evaluate the offer fairly?

## Chapter 8: Retention

1. When the player closes the game after their first session, what unfinished goal or curiosity remains in their mind to bring them back?
2. By day 3, what new content, ability, or challenge has the player encountered that was not visible on day 1?
3. What short-cycle goals (achievable in 5-15 minutes) keep the player engaged within each session? Give 2-3 concrete examples.
4. What stage-unlock or milestone creates an "I need to see what's next" feeling? At what play-time intervals do these appear?
5. After 20+ sessions, what prevents the experience from becoming mechanical repetition? What mastery, variety, or social element sustains engagement?

## Chapter 9: Ad Monetization

1. What ad formats does this game use (rewarded video, interstitial, banner, native)? Which is the primary revenue driver, and which are supplementary?
2. List every rewarded video placement with its trigger moment, what the user receives, expected acceptance rate, and whether viewing is required or optional.
3. Where do interstitial ads appear? What is the minimum interval between them, the maximum per session, and the daily cap per user?
4. What happens when an ad fails to load, the user cancels mid-watch, or there is no ad fill? How do you ensure the player still receives the promised reward?
5. What is your priority order: maximize ad impressions, maximize ad completion rate, or preserve user retention? Where do you draw the line?

## Chapter 10: Light Payments

1. What role does paid content play in this game: supplementary income, high-value user service, experience enhancement, or all three? What is it NOT (e.g., not pay-to-win, not required)?
2. What are your 3-5 IAP products? For each, describe the target buyer, purchase trigger, price range, and expected purchase rate.
3. How is your price ladder structured? What is the lowest entry point, and why does that price feel like a safe first purchase?
4. If you offer a remove-ads package, what exactly does it remove? Do rewarded video placements remain available for users who want them?
5. How do you prevent the paid experience and the ad-supported experience from conflicting? What ensures non-paying users still have a fair experience?

## Chapter 11: Art Style

1. Describe your overall visual style in 3-5 adjectives. For each, explain how it serves the gameplay experience or target audience.
2. What are the main visual elements the player interacts with (characters, objects, tiles, environments)? What are their design constraints (size, color, readability)?
3. What is the emotional tone the art should convey? How does this tone match the core gameplay feeling?
4. What are the key feedback effects (hit, success, failure, reward, combo)? Which effects must be "strongly perceivable" to support gameplay feel?
5. What direction should the game icon, store screenshots, and ad creative materials take? What visual element should immediately communicate what the game is about?

## Chapter 12: UI/UX

1. List your main screens (home, level select, gameplay, results, shop, settings). How does the player navigate between them?
2. For the 3 most important screens, describe the layout priorities: what is the primary action, where are secondary actions, and where are monetization touchpoints?
3. What interaction feedback standards apply? For clicks, drags, successes, failures, locks, and unlocks, what does the player feel/see/hear?
4. Where must ads and payment buttons appear, and what safeguards prevent accidental taps? How is the reward-received state communicated clearly?
5. What are your accessibility and usability baselines: single-hand operation support, minimum tap target size, text readability, color-blind considerations?

## Chapter 13: Tech Requirements

1. What engine or framework do you plan to use, and what are the deciding factors (dev speed, package size, platform support, ad SDK compatibility)?
2. What are your performance targets: frame rate floor, cold start time, maximum package size, and acceptable loading time between levels?
3. What parameters must be server-configurable without releasing an app update (ad frequency, reward values, level parameters, pricing)?
4. What analytics events must be tracked from day one to validate your core design assumptions? List the top 10 events with their trigger conditions.
5. What are your minimum supported devices, OS versions, and screen orientations? How does the game handle weak network or fully offline conditions?

## Chapter 14: Data & Analytics

1. What are your top 5 metrics to monitor daily, ranked by priority? For each, what value range indicates "healthy" vs "needs investigation"?
2. Describe your new-user funnel: what are the 5-7 key steps from download to completing the third session, and where do you expect the biggest drop-offs?
3. What is your ad funnel: impression shown -> clicked -> loaded -> completed -> reward delivered? What completion rate do you target?
4. What is your payment funnel: product seen -> tapped -> payment initiated -> payment completed? What conversion rate do you expect for the first purchase?
5. What are your top 3 A/B test priorities for launch? For each, what is the hypothesis, the variants, the success metric, and the decision threshold?

## Chapter 15: Revenue & ROI

1. What are your primary revenue sources, and what percentage of total revenue do you expect from each (ads vs IAP vs premium)?
2. What are your key ad revenue assumptions: DAU target, daily impressions per user, ad completion rate, average eCPM by region?
3. What are your cost assumptions: development cost, art cost, QA cost, and user acquisition cost for initial validation?
4. Over a 3-month, 6-month, and 12-month horizon, what ROI do you target? Is this a static projection or does it assume ongoing optimization?
5. Describe your optimistic, baseline, and pessimistic scenarios by varying 2-3 key variables. What would trigger a go/no-go decision?
